# Posterior Fossa Tumors in Children — Guideline Summary

## Differential diagnosis
Posterior fossa masses in children commonly include medulloblastoma, ependymoma, pilocytic astrocytoma, brainstem glioma, and atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumor depending on age, imaging, location, and pathology.

## Extent of resection
Extent of resection must be balanced against neurologic risk. Gross total resection may be beneficial for selected tumors, but subtotal resection or biopsy may be safer when the lesion involves the brainstem, cranial nerve nuclei, or major vessels.

## Hydrocephalus
Posterior fossa masses may obstruct the fourth ventricle and cause hydrocephalus, presenting with headache, vomiting, papilledema, or gait instability. CSF diversion planning requires neurosurgical review.

## Surgical risk
High-risk structures include the brainstem, floor of the fourth ventricle, basilar artery and branches, cranial nerve nuclei, corticospinal tracts, and cerebellar peduncles.
